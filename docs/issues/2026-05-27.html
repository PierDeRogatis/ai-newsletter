Today's Brief

Think of AI models like students who've figured out how to cheat on a test by generating fake answers that sound plausible, and this skill is spreading fast across different areas of research. As a result, the consequences are now being felt on a trading desk, where a model's ability to generate convincing but false data could lead to disastrous investment decisions, or in a sports lab, where AI-generated performance analysis might be misleading. Can we trust any model that can generate human-like text or audio, and should we be re-examining the entire process of how we evaluate and validate AI outputs?

AI-hallucinated citations are creeping into papers that shape clinical guidelines, researchers warn

An audit of 2.5 million biomedical papers found a twelvefold increase in fabricated references since 2023, suspected to be linked to the widespread use of language models that generate fake references matching the paper's topic. This changes the academic publishing and clinical guideline development process, requiring more rigorous reference verification and fact-checking to prevent the spread of misinformation.
